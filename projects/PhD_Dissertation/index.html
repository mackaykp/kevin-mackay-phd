Project Overview

Problem

Governments still need to protect children from lead exposure decades after major sources were phased out. Policy and remediation debates often lean on return-on-investment narratives and simplified maps, while soil exposure, dose-response uncertainty, and municipal fiscal reality are not always in the same conversation.

Context

Hamilton, Ontario served as the case: localized soil-lead patterns matter for where children may be at elevated risk; published evidence on blood lead and IQ feeds ROI-style arguments; municipal budgets face hard limits on permanent remediation.

Goals

  • Map and interpret spatial risk using defensible interpolation choices (including a critical look at land-use regression).
  • Quantify blood-lead ↔︎ IQ evidence with modern meta-analysis to scrutinize claims embedded in ROI estimates.
  • Assess abatement costs and feasibility at municipal scale and articulate alternatives when ROI-only framing is misleading.

Stakeholders

  • Supervision and examination: Dr. Bruce Newbold and the doctoral committee (academic standards and contribution).
  • Municipal and public-health audiences implied by the policy-facing chapters.
  • Broader research communities using LUR, soil contamination methods, and meta-analysis.

Final deliverable

The doctoral dissertation (McMaster, 2020)-five substantive chapters plus appendices-and the PDF linked at the bottom of this page. A shorter defense deck is available on the PhD defense presentation page.

Project Approach

Problem Definition

  • Analytical tension (planned): ROI-heavy abatement narratives had often run ahead of integrated evidence on soil pathways, IQ dose-response, and what cities can fund.
  • Research questions (planned): Where in Hamilton might soil-lead imply elevated child exposure-and what do decision-makers need before committing to large-scale dig-and-replace-style programs?
  • Disciplinary stance: pair environmental health modelling with policy-facing economics and hazard communication-not maps or meta-analysis in isolation.

Scope & Assumptions

  • Scope (planned): (1) spatial prediction of soil lead, (2) meta-analysis blood lead ↔︎ IQ, (3) municipal cost-benefit framing.
  • Assumption: linking all three will challenge ROI-only stories while still taking economics seriously.

Data Sources

  • Planned: soil samples + covariates for spatial models; published studies for meta-analysis; Hamilton costs and land-use context for abatement scenarios.

Analytical Design

  • Plan: contrast LUR vs kriging-style options (with a critical lens on LUR).
  • Plan: pool epidemiology with current meta-analytic practice.
  • Plan: connect predictions to hazard-style interpretations decision-makers recognize.

Workflow / Process

Planned sequence: characterize exposure surfaces → quantify dose-response uncertainty → test abatement feasibility and alternatives to permanent dig-and-replace where ROI claims are shaky.

Constraints & Considerations

  • LUR identifiability; study heterogeneity in meta-analysis.
  • Outputs will need to stay actionable under real municipal budgets.

Impact & Outcome

This dissertation provided an integrated evidence base for understanding childhood lead risk in Hamilton and evaluating the feasibility of large-scale abatement. It supports more realistic municipal policy by combining spatial risk modelling, epidemiological synthesis, and cost-benefit critique in a single decision-relevant framework.

Abstract

Thirty years since the removal of lead from gasoline, lead still poses a health risk. Children are most at-risk for adverse health outcomes caused by lead toxicity due to both behavioural (e.g., hand-to-mouth behaviour) and physiological differences (i.e., increased intake of lead by body weight, higher uptake rate and a higher vulnerability to the effects of lead) compared to adults (Yeoh et al., 2009). As a result, governments must identify children that may be at-risk of lead poisoning and develop practical methods to mitigate lead exposure.

Before a government can develop a policy to help mitigate exposure of lead for children, we need to understand the spatial distribution of lead within the city. A popular spatial model used within air pollution research may allow more accurate, and more localized predictions than the most common interpolation method, kriging. Land use regression (LUR) is a technique leveraging multiple predictor variables to help estimate the spatial distribution of the dependent variable. By using historical sources of lead, LUR can be used to model soil lead levels (SLL) with localized variation. Unfortunately, spurious relationships can be the basis of a LUR model, which may lead to an overfitted spatial model resulting in a model with little generalizability and questionable ability to estimate the dependent variable at unobserved locations. Ultimately, Empirical Bayesian Kriging may be the best option for soil contamination research due to its ability to provide a smoothed prediction surface and its dependence on the spatial structure of the data to provide estimations.

The benefit to society and the return on investment (ROI) is often the justification for lead remediation. Gould (2009) estimates a $17 to $221 ROI for every dollar spent on lead hazard control. One of the main components of this estimate of ROI comes from the decrease in intelligence quotient (IQ) that a child may experience as a consequence of lead toxicity. There are three main ways that a decrease in IQ can negatively impact the economy, (i) lower potential lifetime earnings, (ii) reduced tax revenues, and (iii) higher spending on special education (Gould, 2009). Since IQ has such a significant role in the ROI estimates, chapter 3 seeks to achieve a greater understanding of the relationship between blood lead levels (BLLs) and IQ. The loss of IQ points for an increase in blood lead concentration proposed by Lanphear et al. (2005) and referenced by Gould (2009) is significantly higher than what we found in our meta-analysis. Thus, the projected ROI proposed by Gould (2009) may be much lower than previously calculated.

In the final chapter, the cost associated with permanent lead abatement is investigated based on ROI projections as a case study in Hamilton, Ontario. We show that, in most cases, permanent lead remediation is far too expensive for a municipal government. Furthermore, the capital initially invested may not be distributed back into the local economy, as the ROI suggests. We suggest that municipal governments make decisions based on need, rather than basing remediation decisions on ROI projections. Furthermore, we recommend the use of hazard quotient maps to help justify lead remediation as a more accurate representation of potential lead toxicity, instead of only looking at SLL exceedances across the city.

Preface

This thesis dissertation contains five chapters: an introduction, three main chapters, and a conclusion.

  • Chapter 2 examines a relatively new interpolation method for heavy metal soil contamination research and discusses the inherent flaws in using land use regression compared to the traditional method of kriging.
  • Chapter 3 is a meta-analysis exploring the relationship between blood lead levels in children and intelligence quotient (IQ), presenting a thorough investigation using modern meta-analysis techniques to synthesize unstandardized beta coefficients.
  • Chapter 4 presents a cost-benefit analysis of lead remediation in Hamilton, consisting of soil, water, and paint abatement. Despite significant return on investment projections, the paper discusses reasons why a city should reconsider investing in such a large project.

The first chapter is a methodological paper that contributes to a broader research audience (e.g., air pollution research) that uses land use regression for spatial modelling. The second chapter contributes to the literature by quantifying the relationship between blood lead levels in children and intelligence quotient by pooling results from 13 papers. Finally, the last paper contributes by examining the feasibility of lead remediation at a municipal level and discussing alternatives to permanent lead abatement. This dissertation seeks to challenge the necessity and benefit of lead remediation in urban cities by providing a case study in Hamilton, Ontario.
