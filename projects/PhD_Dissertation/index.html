Project Overview

Problem

Thirty years since the removal of lead from gasoline, lead still poses a health risk. Children are most at-risk for adverse health outcomes caused by lead toxicity due to both behavioural and physiological differences compared to adults, so governments must identify children that may be at-risk of lead poisoning and develop practical methods to mitigate lead exposure.

Context

This dissertation focuses on Hamilton, Ontario as a case study because Hamilton is a relatively old city with old housing stock and a long industrial history that may increase exposure to lead within the city. The work builds on Richardson et al. (2011), with added emphasis on continuous spatial modelling, the relationship between blood lead levels and IQ, and the real-world feasibility of permanent lead abatement at a municipal level.

Goals

  • Identify geographic areas in Hamilton where children may be at-risk of lead poisoning and characterize sources of lead that may increase exposure risk.
  • Determine whether land use regression is a viable alternative to kriging for estimating soil lead levels, or whether Empirical Bayesian Kriging is the stronger approach.
  • Quantify the relationship between childhood blood lead levels and IQ using meta-analysis techniques to synthesize unstandardized beta coefficients.
  • Assess the projected cost and return on investment of permanent lead hazard control, and evaluate need-based alternatives for municipal decision-making.

Stakeholders

  • Academic stakeholders: doctoral supervision and examination at McMaster University, School of Earth, Environment, & Society (formerly School of Geography and Earth Sciences).
  • Public health and municipal audiences: decision-makers evaluating lead exposure mitigation and remediation strategies in urban settings.
  • Research communities: scholars working in spatial interpolation, environmental health, and meta-analysis.

Project Approach

Problem Definition

  • Research problem: Despite declining blood lead levels, lead remains a problem in urban Canada, and low blood lead concentrations can still produce adverse outcomes in children.
  • Methodological problem: Soil lead is continuous across space, so neighbourhood-level summaries can obscure local variation, introduce abrupt boundary effects, and limit risk identification.
  • Decision problem: Return-on-investment arguments for permanent lead abatement are influential, but municipalities must also evaluate feasibility, local budget constraints, and need-based prioritization.

Scope & Assumptions

  • Scope: The dissertation contains an introduction, three main analytical chapters, and a conclusion: Chapter 2 on interpolation and soil lead modelling, Chapter 3 on blood lead-IQ meta-analysis, and Chapter 4 on cost-benefit and hazard-based policy assessment.
  • Assumption: Integrated evidence across spatial modelling, epidemiological synthesis, and municipal cost analysis provides a stronger basis for policy than any single method in isolation.

Data Sources

  • Spatial analysis inputs: 187 soil lead samples from Hamilton Public Health Services (2008-2009), historical business records, and a modified historical road network.
  • Meta-analysis inputs: Published studies quantifying the relationship between childhood blood lead levels and IQ.
  • Policy and feasibility inputs: Hamilton-focused assumptions and cost components for soil, water, and paint abatement, with hazard quotient mapping to evaluate potential toxicity risk.

Analytical Design

  • Chapter 2 design: Critically assess land use regression against Ordinary Kriging and Empirical Bayesian Kriging for soil lead interpolation in an urban setting.
  • Chapter 3 design: Use modern meta-analysis methods to synthesize unstandardized beta coefficients for blood lead and IQ, including heterogeneity and bias diagnostics.
  • Chapter 4 design: Estimate cost and ROI for permanent lead hazard control, then pair financial results with hazard quotient maps to evaluate practical need and policy direction.

Workflow / Process

  1. Characterize historical lead sources and model the spatial distribution of soil lead in Hamilton.
  2. Evaluate dose-response evidence by meta-analyzing childhood blood lead-IQ relationships.
  3. Estimate the cost and projected ROI of permanent lead abatement (soil, paint, and lead service pipes).
  4. Assess potential threat using hazard quotient maps and discuss alternatives to permanent abatement under municipal budget constraints.

Constraints & Considerations

  • LUR models can be overfitted when spurious relationships are used as predictors.
  • Meta-analysis must account for between-study heterogeneity, influence, and publication bias.
  • Municipal governments face substantial fiscal constraints, even where projected social returns appear large.

Abstract

Thirty years since the removal of lead from gasoline, lead still poses a health risk. Children are most at-risk for adverse health outcomes caused by lead toxicity due to both behavioural (e.g., hand-to-mouth behaviour) and physiological differences (i.e., increased intake of lead by body weight, higher uptake rate and a higher vulnerability to the effects of lead) compared to adults (Yeoh et al., 2009). As a result, governments must identify children that may be at-risk of lead poisoning and develop practical methods to mitigate lead exposure.

Before a government can develop a policy to help mitigate exposure of lead for children, we need to understand the spatial distribution of lead within the city. A popular spatial model used within air pollution research may allow more accurate, and more localized predictions than the most common interpolation method, kriging. Land use regression (LUR) is a technique leveraging multiple predictor variables to help estimate the spatial distribution of the dependent variable. By using historical sources of lead, LUR can be used to model soil lead levels (SLL) with localized variation. Unfortunately, spurious relationships can be the basis of a LUR model, which may lead to an overfitted spatial model resulting in a model with little generalizability and questionable ability to estimate the dependent variable at unobserved locations. Ultimately, Empirical Bayesian Kriging may be the best option for soil contamination research due to its ability to provide a smoothed prediction surface and its dependence on the spatial structure of the data to provide estimations.

The benefit to society and the return on investment (ROI) is often the justification for lead remediation. Gould (2009) estimates a $17 to $221 ROI for every dollar spent on lead hazard control. One of the main components of this estimate of ROI comes from the decrease in intelligence quotient (IQ) that a child may experience as a consequence of lead toxicity. There are three main ways that a decrease in IQ can negatively impact the economy, (i) lower potential lifetime earnings, (ii) reduced tax revenues, and (iii) higher spending on special education (Gould, 2009). Since IQ has such a significant role in the ROI estimates, chapter 3 seeks to achieve a greater understanding of the relationship between blood lead levels (BLLs) and IQ. The loss of IQ points for an increase in blood lead concentration proposed by Lanphear et al. (2005) and referenced by Gould (2009) is significantly higher than what we found in our meta-analysis. Thus, the projected ROI proposed by Gould (2009) may be much lower than previously calculated.

In the final chapter, the cost associated with permanent lead abatement is investigated based on ROI projections as a case study in Hamilton, Ontario. We show that, in most cases, permanent lead remediation is far too expensive for a municipal government. Furthermore, the capital initially invested may not be distributed back into the local economy, as the ROI suggests. We suggest that municipal governments make decisions based on need, rather than basing remediation decisions on ROI projections. Furthermore, we recommend the use of hazard quotient maps to help justify lead remediation as a more accurate representation of potential lead toxicity, instead of only looking at SLL exceedances across the city.

Preface

This thesis dissertation contains five chapters: an introduction, three main chapters, and a conclusion.

  • Chapter 2: examines a relatively new interpolation method for heavy metal soil contamination research and discusses the inherent flaws in using land use regression compared to the traditional method of kriging.
  • Chapter 3: is a meta-analysis exploring the relationship between blood lead levels in children and intelligence quotient (IQ), presenting a thorough investigation using modern meta-analysis techniques to synthesize unstandardized beta coefficients.
  • Chapter 4: presents a cost-benefit analysis of lead remediation in Hamilton, consisting of soil, water, and paint abatement. Despite significant return on investment projections, the paper discusses reasons why a city should reconsider investing in such a large project.

The first chapter is a methodological paper that contributes to a broader research audience (e.g., air pollution research) that uses land use regression for spatial modelling. The second chapter contributes to the literature by quantifying the relationship between blood lead levels in children and intelligence quotient by pooling results from 13 papers. Finally, the last paper contributes by examining the feasibility of lead remediation at a municipal level and discussing alternatives to permanent lead abatement. This dissertation seeks to challenge the necessity and benefit of lead remediation in urban cities by providing a case study in Hamilton, Ontario.

Impact & Outcome

This dissertation provided an integrated evidence base for identifying where children may be at-risk of lead poisoning in Hamilton and for reassessing how municipalities evaluate lead remediation policy. The findings show that Empirical Bayesian Kriging may be preferable to land use regression for this soil contamination context, that estimated IQ loss per blood lead increase may be smaller than values often used in ROI arguments, and that permanent city-wide lead abatement can be prohibitively expensive for municipal governments. Taken together, the work supports decisions based on demonstrated need and hazard-informed targeting, rather than ROI projections alone.
