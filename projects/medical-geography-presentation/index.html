Health Statistics From A Geographer’s Perspective

Branch-facing medical geography primer built to introduce geospatial analysis capabilities, using practical health examples that work for mixed technical audiences.

Medical geography synthesis Simplifying technical methods Teaching presentation

A supervisor-requested presentation for Statistics Canada’s Health Statistics branch, developed to explain what geospatial analysis and GIS can contribute to ongoing branch projects. The deck is intentionally structured as an accessible bridge - fundamentals first, then concrete method families and examples - rather than as a full spatial-statistics course.

Project Overview

Problem

While working in the Health Statistics branch at Statistics Canada, I was asked by my supervisor to deliver a branch-wide presentation introducing the type of spatial analysis and GIS work I could support. Spatial methods were not widely represented in the branch, and most teams were strong in health content but had limited exposure to medical geography workflows.

Context

The presentation was designed for a mixed audience: people with deep institutional and subject-matter knowledge, but a broad range of comfort with statistics and spatial data. The goal was to make methods understandable without oversimplifying them, while still giving advanced staff enough technical depth to see where these approaches could strengthen their own projects.

Goals

  • Introduce medical geography as a practical framework for health-statistics work, not just a niche specialty.
  • Build from fundamentals (GIS preprocessing, geocoding, and visualization) into pattern analysis, clustering, prediction modeling, and accessibility methods.
  • Show how these methods can support complex research questions and project design decisions inside the branch.

Stakeholders

  • Primary audience: Health Statistics branch staff and analysts at Statistics Canada.
  • Presenter role: One of the limited in-branch practitioners focused on spatial analysis and GIS support.

Final deliverable

The slide deck (PDF) introduces medical geography fundamentals, then moves through concrete method families and examples: preprocessing and geocoding, visualization choices, spatial autocorrelation (including Moran’s I and LISA), clustering approaches, predictive modeling (for example MGWR, random forest, geographical random forest, kriging, and land-use regression), hazard quotient mapping, and accessibility analysis (suitability, location-allocation, service areas, and 2SFCA).

Project Approach

Problem Definition

  • Context: Branch leadership wanted a clear introduction to what geospatial analysis could contribute to health statistics projects and where that expertise could be applied.
  • What was needed: A practical, teaching-oriented structure that could connect broad concepts to recognizable project tasks and help non-specialists identify when to engage spatial methods.

Scope & Assumptions

  • Scope: Introductory-to-intermediate coverage, from foundational GIS processing through selected advanced methods, framed for branch use cases rather than as a full graduate-level methods course.
  • Audience assumption: Participants would vary widely in technical background, so explanations needed plain-language framing first, followed by technical examples that specialists could still engage with.

Data Sources

  • Examples in the deck: Conceptual and applied health-geography examples from published or common practice contexts, including point, line, and areal mapping scenarios, geocoding cases, and model comparison illustrations used to explain method choice.

Analytical Design

  • Instructional flow: fundamentals first (medical geography concepts, preprocessing, geocoding, visualization) followed by method families for pattern detection, clustering, prediction, and accessibility.
  • Pattern analysis block: spatial autocorrelation and local pattern interpretation (including Moran’s I and LISA), then clustering approaches (k-means, fuzzy c-means, and local geographically weighted variants).
  • Prediction and accessibility blocks: regression and machine-learning examples (including MGWR and geographical random forest), interpolation and exposure mapping concepts (including kriging and hazard quotient mapping), and planning-oriented accessibility methods (suitability, location-allocation, service areas, 2SFCA).

Workflow / Process

  1. Frame the objective: what medical geography is and why spatial relationships matter for health questions.
  2. Introduce data preparation and geocoding so downstream analysis choices are grounded in data quality and geometry.
  3. Walk through visualization and pattern-analysis methods, emphasizing interpretation over formula memorization.
  4. Extend to predictive and accessibility methods with concrete examples to show decision support value.
  5. Close with broader method inventory and collaboration opportunities so teams can map ideas back to their own projects.

Constraints & Considerations

  • The audience included both non-specialists and technical staff, so pacing and terminology had to stay accessible without flattening methodological nuance.
  • Session time required selective depth: enough detail to make methods actionable, but not so much that the presentation became a full methods course.
  • Examples emphasized interpretation boundaries and responsible use, especially when moving from exploratory mapping to predictive claims.

Impact & Outcome

The presentation was well received and increased awareness of medical geography across the branch. Colleagues shared that it was their first structured exposure to how spatial analysis and spatial statistics can support health-statistics work, and several teams began exploring where these methods could improve their own projects.

It also clarified where my geospatial expertise could be brought into branch initiatives, creating a clearer collaboration path between health-domain specialists and spatial-method support.
