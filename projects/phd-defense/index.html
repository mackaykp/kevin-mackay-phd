Doctoral defense slides (McMaster, 2020): a summary of the dissertation findings across soil lead risk in Hamilton, interpolation method assessment, blood lead-IQ evidence, and municipal lead-abatement feasibility. Project Overview

Problem

Thirty years since the removal of lead from gasoline, lead still poses a health risk. Children are most at-risk for adverse health outcomes caused by lead toxicity due to both behavioural and physiological differences compared to adults, so governments must identify children that may be at-risk of lead poisoning and develop practical methods to mitigate lead exposure.

Context

This defense presentation summarizes the dissertation case study in Hamilton, Ontario, where old housing stock and long industrial history may increase exposure to lead. It consolidates three dissertation components into one defense narrative: spatial modelling of soil lead, meta-analysis of blood lead levels and IQ, and municipal feasibility of permanent lead abatement.

Goals

  • Present a clear summary of dissertation objectives, methods, and findings during the oral defense.
  • Defend the methodological findings on land use regression, kriging, and meta-analysis decisions.
  • Explain policy implications for municipal lead remediation, including budget constraints and need-based prioritization.

Stakeholders

  • Defense committee: Dr. Bruce Newbold, Dr. Darren Scott, Dr. Niko Yiannakoulias, and Dr. Eric Crighton (external committee member).
  • Academic audience: faculty, research staff and students attending the defense.
  • Municipal and public-health audiences: decision-makers reflected in the policy-facing implications of the dissertation.

Final deliverable

The final deliverable is the PhD defense slide deck (PDF), presented during a three-hour defense as a structured summary of the full dissertation and used to defend the research findings before the examination committee. The complete dissertation is available on the main PhD dissertation page.

Project Approach

Problem Definition

  • Research problem: Despite declining blood lead levels, lead remains a problem in urban Canada, and low blood lead concentrations can still produce adverse outcomes in children.
  • Methodological problem: Soil lead is continuous across space, so neighbourhood-level summaries can obscure local variation and limit risk identification.
  • Decision problem: Return-on-investment arguments for permanent lead abatement are influential, but municipalities must also evaluate feasibility, local budget constraints, and need-based prioritization.

Scope & Assumptions

  • Scope: A defense summary aligned to dissertation chapters, covering Chapter 2 interpolation findings, Chapter 3 meta-analysis findings, and Chapter 4 cost-benefit and hazard-based policy findings.
  • Assumption: A single integrated presentation can communicate methodological rigor and policy relevance clearly to a multidisciplinary committee.

Data Sources

  • Spatial analysis inputs: 187 soil lead samples from Hamilton Public Health Services (2008-2009), historical business records, and a modified historical road network.
  • Meta-analysis inputs: Published studies used to quantify the relationship between childhood blood lead levels and IQ.
  • Policy and feasibility inputs: Hamilton-focused cost and ROI assumptions for soil, water, and paint abatement, with hazard quotient mapping.

Analytical Design

  • Chapter 2 summary: Critical assessment of land use regression against Ordinary Kriging and Empirical Bayesian Kriging for soil lead interpolation.
  • Chapter 3 summary: Meta-analysis synthesis of blood lead-IQ relationships, including heterogeneity and bias diagnostics.
  • Chapter 4 summary: Cost and ROI assessment for permanent lead hazard control, paired with hazard quotient evidence to evaluate practical need.

Workflow / Process

  1. Introduce dissertation motivation, objectives, and Hamilton case context.
  2. Present spatial modelling findings on soil lead and interpolation method performance.
  3. Present meta-analysis findings on blood lead levels and IQ.
  4. Present cost-benefit and hazard quotient findings for municipal lead abatement policy.
  5. Defend interpretations and implications through committee questioning in the oral examination.

Constraints & Considerations

  • The defense had to summarize a full dissertation in a single two-hour examination.
  • Methodological and policy claims needed to remain evidence-based and clearly bounded.
  • The presentation had to address both technical critique and practical municipal implications.

Impact & Outcome

This defense presentation translated the dissertation into a coherent oral examination narrative and supported successful defense of the research across spatial modelling, epidemiological synthesis, and policy feasibility. It clarified that Empirical Bayesian Kriging may be preferable to land use regression in this context, that blood lead-IQ effect sizes may yield lower projected ROI than commonly cited estimates, and that permanent city-wide lead abatement can be prohibitively expensive for municipal governments. Taken together, the defense emphasized need-based and hazard-informed decision-making rather than ROI projections alone.
